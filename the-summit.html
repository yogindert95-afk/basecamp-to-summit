The route
The problem

At the top, the problems get quieter — and heavier.

Senior leaders rarely get coached. The higher you climb, the less honest feedback reaches you and the more each decision carries — succession, culture, the careers of the managers beneath you.

The Summit is about leading at altitude — strategy, influence without authority, and building the leaders who come after you.

Who this is for

The Summit is deliberately small.

The leader of leaders

You manage managers now. Your leverage is no longer your own work — it's how well the people below you lead. You need coaching for problems you can't discuss with your own team.

The senior team that isn't one

A leadership team of strong individuals who don't yet operate as one unit — guarded meetings, decisions relitigated in corridors. We work with the team together, on the real dynamics.

The exec sponsor of succession

You're accountable for who leads next — a founder, CXO, or business head who needs a deliberate bench, not a hope that the next layer figures it out in time.

Questions at the Summit

Before you rope up.

Is this coaching or training?+

Both, deliberately. Group sessions build shared language across your senior team; 1:1 executive work goes where a group can't — your specific decisions, relationships, and blind spots.

How confidential is the 1:1 work?+

Fully. What's discussed in 1:1s stays there — sponsors get themes and progress, never transcripts. Senior leaders only open up when that's true, so it's non-negotiable.

Who's in the room?+

Peers only — a senior team of 6 to 12, or a cross-company cohort at equivalent altitude. No one is managing anyone else in the room, which changes what can be said.

What does the arc look like?+

Usually a few intensive sessions with spaced 1:1s across three to six months. Leading at altitude is a practice, not an event — the spacing is where the change happens.
